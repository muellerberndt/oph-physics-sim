# Cosmic Microwave Background as an OPH observable

The CMB is the cleanest large-scale “record surface” we have. In standard cosmology, it is the relic photon bath from the hot early universe. In OPH language, it is the oldest high-fidelity boundary packet currently entering our observer patch: photons arrive now, are written into present detectors, and encode conditions on the last-scattering surface plus the gravitational history along the line of sight.

The important split is this:

**The microwaves themselves do not need an exotic OPH generation mechanism.** If OPH keeps the recovered Standard Model / electromagnetic branch intact, then the CMB photons are ordinary photons from a thermal photon-baryon plasma.

**The OPH pressure point is the stress sector those photons probe.** The CMB acoustic peaks, CMB lensing, BAO, growth, and (S_8) test whether OPH’s effective dark stress can behave like the gravitating component that standard (\Lambda)CDM models as cold dark matter. The OPH dark-sector paper explicitly treats cosmology as a third claim layer: the same anomaly stress must enter linear perturbation theory with a background abundance, relaxation rate, and equilibrium kernel; CMB, BAO, lensing, growth, and (S_8) are the tests. 

## 1. How were the microwaves generated?

In the usual early-universe theory, the CMB photons began as part of a hot, dense, nearly thermal plasma of photons, electrons, protons, helium nuclei, neutrinos, and gravitational perturbations. Before recombination, photons scattered rapidly from free electrons by Thomson scattering, so radiation and baryons behaved as a coupled fluid. Compression by gravitational wells and photon pressure produced acoustic oscillations. When the universe cooled enough for electrons and nuclei to form neutral atoms, the free-electron density collapsed, the mean free path of photons became cosmological, and photons decoupled.

NASA’s WMAP overview states the CMB radiation was released roughly (375{,}000) years after the universe began, and that WMAP mapped tiny temperature differences over the full sky. It gives representative temperatures of (2.7251) K and (2.7249) K in different directions. ([NASA Science][1]) ESA’s Planck overview describes the CMB as the relic radiation from the Big Bang, measured today at about (2.7) K, with anisotropies at the level of one part in (100{,}000). ([European Space Agency][2])

Strictly speaking, the photons were not “microwaves” at emission. At last scattering the radiation temperature was about (3000) K, so the spectrum was much hotter. Cosmic expansion stretched every photon wavelength by roughly (1+z_\ast\sim 1100), preserving the blackbody form while lowering the temperature to today’s (T_0\simeq2.725) K. In formula form,

[
T(z)=T_0(1+z),
\qquad
\nu_0=\frac{\nu_\ast}{1+z_\ast}.
]

So the correct generation story is: **thermal photons were released at recombination, then redshifted into microwaves.**

## 3. What should we measure in our subjective present?

As present observers, we should not expect to see “the early universe as it is now.” We see photons crossing our local boundary now, whose last major scattering events lie on our past light cone. The expected data product is a set of present records:

[
T(\hat n,\nu),\quad Q(\hat n,\nu),\quad U(\hat n,\nu),
]

where (T) is temperature and (Q,U) encode linear polarization. After foreground cleaning and calibration, these are compressed into angular spectra:

[
C_\ell^{TT},\quad C_\ell^{TE},\quad C_\ell^{EE},\quad C_\ell^{BB},
\quad C_\ell^{\phi\phi}
]

for temperature, polarization, and lensing.

The expected measurements are:

A nearly perfect blackbody monopole at (T_0\simeq2.725) K; a large dipole mostly from our motion relative to the CMB rest frame; primordial temperature anisotropies at (\Delta T/T\sim10^{-5}); acoustic peaks in (TT), (TE), and (EE); lensing-induced smoothing and a reconstructable lensing potential; and foreground contamination from the Galaxy and extragalactic sources that must be modeled or removed. ESA describes Planck’s role as mapping those anisotropies across microwave to far-infrared bands, and notes that they are the imprints of seeds of today’s galaxies and clusters. ([European Space Agency][2])

Under standard (\Lambda)CDM, Planck 2018 found consistency with a flat six-parameter model using temperature, polarization, and lensing. The combined analysis gives, among other parameters,

[
\Omega_c h^2=0.120\pm0.001,\quad
\Omega_b h^2=0.0224\pm0.0001,\quad
n_s=0.965\pm0.004,
]

and an angular acoustic scale measured to (0.03%), with (100\theta_\ast=1.0411\pm0.0003). ([arXiv][3])

In OPH, the corresponding expectation is: the recovered GR/electromagnetic sector should reproduce the ordinary photon-baryon acoustic physics, while the OPH anomaly sector must reproduce the gravitational role normally assigned to cold dark matter, or else fail. The OPH dark paper lists as a falsifier the case where CMB peaks, CMB lensing, BAO, weak lensing, or growth reject every (\Gamma_{\rm rec}), (B_A(k,a)), and (\rho_A(a)) closure compatible with the galaxy branch.

Pure theory can say:

[

A practical OPH-CMB simulation should be built in layers.

### Layer A — Standard photon-baryon-recombination baseline

Use a standard Boltzmann code such as CAMB or CLASS. Keep the ordinary photon, baryon, electron, neutrino, and recombination physics intact unless OPH explicitly changes the electromagnetic or particle branch.

The standard visibility function is

[
g(\eta)=-\tau'(\eta)e^{-\tau(\eta)},
]

where (\tau) is optical depth. This function selects the finite thickness of the last-scattering surface. The output should reproduce standard (TT), (TE), (EE), and lensing spectra in the (\Lambda)CDM limit before any OPH extension is trusted.
